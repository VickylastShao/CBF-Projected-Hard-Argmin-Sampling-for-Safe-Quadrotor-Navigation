# Non-Linear Model Predictive Control Based on Probabilistic Tiny Recursive Models: A Test-Time Compute Scaling Approach for Quadrotor 3D Obstacle Avoidance (May 26, 2026, 23:55)

## Abstract

When steering underactuated systems through complex non-convex obstacle fields, the real-time online implementation of Nonlinear Model Predictive Control (NMPC) is constrained by limited onboard computation and local-minimum sensitivity. This paper proposes **PTRM-NMPC** (Probabilistic Tiny Recursive Model-based Nonlinear Model Predictive Control), a compact approximate predictive-control framework that adapts the idea of test-time compute scaling to safe quadrotor guidance. The proposed controller uses a weight-shared latent recursive network with $27{,}935$ trainable parameters (approximately $28\text{K}$) to approximate the implicit NMPC policy over a 6-dimensional translational outer-loop abstraction derived from a 12-dimensional quadrotor model. At inference time, clamped Gaussian perturbations are injected into the recursive latent dynamics to generate $K$ candidate control sequences, and a co-trained regression $Q$-head ranks these candidates using rollout costs aligned with the nominal NMPC objective. A discrete-time composite control barrier function (DT-CCBF) projection and a box-constrained fallback policy are used as a safety filter under input saturation. Under explicit bounded-disturbance, feasibility, and linearization-residual assumptions, the closed-loop analysis establishes practical safety and practical input-to-state stability (pISS) rather than exact global invariance. The accompanying Monte Carlo script reports obstacle-avoidance, tracking, robustness, and runtime metrics directly at execution time, so the numerical tables in this manuscript should be filled from a complete run of the provided code on the target hardware.

## 1\. Introduction

Nonlinear Model Predictive Control (NMPC) has established itself as a premier methodology for safety-critical feedback control of high-performance cyber-physical systems (CPS), such as unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs), autonomous vehicles, and robotic manipulators \[1\], \[2\]. Its primary advantage lies in the systematic handling of multi-variable coupling, complex nonlinear dynamics, and hard state/input constraints by solving a finite-horizon optimal control problem (OCP) at each sampling instant \[2\], \[12\]. However, the real-time execution of NMPC remains a grand challenge, especially in high-dimensional or highly non-convex environments. For a quadrotor traversing a dense 3D obstacle field, the non-convex constraints imposed by the obstacles introduce multiple local minima in the optimization landscape (e.g., being blocked directly behind a large obstacle). Under such circumstances, standard numerical optimization solvers (e.g., sequential quadratic programming SQP, interior-point methods, active-set methods) are not only computationally expensive, often violating the millisecond-level execution constraints, but are also highly prone to getting trapped in local minima \[1\], \[12\].

To alleviate this real-time computational bottleneck, Approximate MPC has emerged as a promising alternative \[3\], \[4\]. The core idea is to train a Deep Neural Network (DNN) offline to learn the implicit control policy generated by computationally heavy expert solvers, requiring only computationally cheap neural network forward passes during online execution \[5\], \[6\], \[7\]. However, traditional approximate NMPC schemes suffer from two fundamental limitations \[3\], \[9\]:

1. **Robustness Vulnerability of Single-Pass Mappings ("One-Step-to-Death")**: Traditional approximate controllers, such as Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs) or standard Transformers, operate as static, single-pass mappings \[8\]. When encountering Out-Of-Distribution (OOD) wind disturbances or boundary conditions near obstacles, the single-pass approximation error can easily push the system state into the "bad basins" of the cost function. Lacking any online correction, iterative refinement, or active escape mechanisms, the system is susceptible to severe oscillations or catastrophic collisions \[9\].
2. **Conflict Between Parameter Footprint and Approximation Accuracy**: Accurately fitting high-dimensional, non-smooth control laws typically requires stacking deep and wide network layers. This leads to millions of parameters, which severely hinders deployment on resource-constrained micro-controller units (MCUs) typically found on small quadrotors.

To break these bottlenecks, we propose a novel **PTRM-NMPC** framework, inspired by the recently proposed Tiny Recursive Models (TRMs) \[13\], Probabilistic Tiny Recursive Models (PTRMs) \[14\], and the concept of test-time compute scaling in LLMs \[15\].

- **At the architectural level**, we design an ultra-compact, weight-shared recursive network in the latent space with only $28\text{K}$ **(27,935 trainable parameters)**. By reusing projection and transition weights, the network performs $D$ recursive inference steps in the depth direction, aiming to improve policy approximation while keeping a small parameter footprint.
- **At the algorithmic level**, we introduce **Test-Time Compute Scaling** into real-time control. During online inference, we inject bounded clamped Gaussian noise into the latent state across the recursive steps, generating $K$ parallel candidate rollouts (Width Scaling).
- **At the selection level**, a co-trained $Q$-head regression network evaluates the actual trajectory cost of all $K$ candidates in milliseconds, selecting the best action. This stochastic exploration is intended to improve escape from metastable local attractors via non-equilibrium random drift.
- **At the safety level**, we incorporate a smooth discrete-time composite Control Barrier Function (DT-CCBF) and a discontinuous fallback safety policy to resolve the conflicts between control saturation and safety. We analyze practical invariance, self-healing behavior, and practical Input-to-State Stability (pISS) under explicitly stated feasibility, bounded-disturbance, and switching assumptions.

### 1.1 Notations

$\mathbb{R}^n$ denotes the $n$-dimensional real Euclidean space; $\|\cdot\|_2$ denotes the standard Euclidean $l_2$ norm; for a symmetric positive definite matrix $P$, $\|x\|_P^2$ is defined as $x^T P x$; $\mathcal{C}$ denotes the closed safe state space, with its topological boundary denoted by $\partial \mathcal{C}$; $\operatorname{clip}(\cdot,a,b)$ denotes element-wise saturation to the interval $[a,b]$.

## 2\. Problem Formulation & Preliminaries

Consider a 12-dimensional underactuated quadrotor unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) system modeled as:

$$
x_{12d, k+1} = g_{12d}(x_{12d, k}, u_{12d, k}) \tag{2.1}
$$

where $x_{12d, k} = [p_k^T, \eta_k^T, v_k^T, \omega_k^T]^T \in \mathcal{X}_{12d} \subset \mathbb{R}^{12}$ is the full physical state vector. $p_k = [x_k, y_k, z_k]^T$ denotes the 3D position, $\eta_k = [\phi_k, \theta_k, \psi_k]^T$ represents the Euler angles (roll, pitch, yaw), $v_k = [v_{x,k}, v_{y,k}, v_{z,k}]^T$ represents the linear velocities, and $\omega_k = [p_k, q_k, r_k]^T$ represents the body angular velocities. The physical control input is $u_{12d, k} = [f_k, \tau_{x,k}, \tau_{y,k}, \tau_{z,k}]^T \in \mathcal{U}_{12d} \subset \mathbb{R}^{4}$ comprising the total thrust $f$ and the 3D control moments $\tau$. Under standard physical configurations, the continuous-time dynamics are control-affine and can be expressed as:

$$
\dot{p} = v, \quad \dot{\eta} = W(\eta) \omega
$$
 
$$
\dot{v} = \frac{f}{m} R(\eta) e_3 - g_{grav} e_3 - \frac{1}{m} K_d v
$$
 
$$
\dot{\omega} = J^{-1} (\tau - \omega \times J \omega)
$$

where $m$ is the mass, $R(\eta)$ is the $Z-Y-X$ rotation matrix, $e_3 = [0, 0, 1]^T$, $K_d$ is the diagonal drag coefficient matrix, $g_{grav}$ is the gravitational acceleration, and $J = \text{diag}(I_{xx}, I_{yy}, I_{zz})$ is the inertia tensor. $\mathcal{X}_{12d}$ and $\mathcal{U}_{12d}$ are known compact physical constraint sets representing the hard boundaries.

### A. Nominal and Perturbed Abstraction Models (Guidance Subsystem)

For outer-loop guidance and safety barrier design, the translational motion is abstracted into a 6D state-space representation where the translational state vector is defined as $x_k = [p_k^T, v_k^T]^T \in \mathcal{X} \subset \mathbb{R}^6$. The virtual control input $u_k \in \mathcal{U} \subset \mathbb{R}^3$ represents the gravity-precompensated net force command applied to the quadrotor center of mass. The physical gravity term is handled later by the flatness mapping into total thrust, so it is not subtracted again in the outer-loop abstraction. The input is subject to symmetric actuator saturation constraints:

$$
\mathcal{U} = \{u \in \mathbb{R}^3 \mid u_{min} \le u_i \le u_{max}, \forall i=1,2,3\}
$$

The actual perturbed discrete-time state-space dynamics of this outer-loop guidance subsystem are formulated as:

$$
x_{k+1} = g(x_k, u_k) = A_d x_k + B_d u_k + w_k + d_k \tag{2.2}
$$

where $w_k \in \mathbb{R}^6$ represents persistent bounded wind gust disturbances with $\|w_k\|_2 \le \delta_w$, and $d_k \in \mathbb{R}^6$ is the attitude tracking lag perturbation. The nominal unperturbed dynamics $g_0(x_k, u_k)$ are defined as:

$$
x_{k+1} = g_0(x_k, u_k) = A_d x_k + B_d u_k \tag{2.3}
$$

### B. Cascaded Flight Architecture and Flatness Mapping

Since a quadrotor is an underactuated system, we utilize a cascaded architecture to map the virtual control input $u_k \in \mathbb{R}^3$ into physical commands.

```markdown
[Position/Vel SP] --> [Outer PTRM-NMPC] --(Virtual Force u)--> [Differential Flatness]
                                                                        |
                                                               (Thrust T, Attitude η)
                                                                        |
[Actual State x] <--- [Quadrotor Dynamics] <--- [Inner High-Freq Attitude Ctrl] <-------'
```

**Lemma 1 (Cascaded Disturbance Bounding & Differential Flatness).** *Let the outer-loop safety controller (PTRM-NMPC) output the gravity-precompensated desired virtual force* $u_k^{des}$ *. Define the physical desired thrust vector as* $F_k^{des} \triangleq u_k^{des} + m g_{grav} e_3$ *. Via differential flatness mapping, this vector can be translated into the desired total thrust* $T_k^{des}$ *and desired attitude* $\eta_k^{des} = [\phi_k^{des}, \theta_k^{des}, \psi_k^{des}]^T$ *under a prescribed yaw* $\psi_k$ *as follows:*

$$
T_k^{des} = \|F_k^{des}\|_2, \quad
\phi_k^{des} = \arcsin\left(\frac{F_{x,k}^{des}\sin\psi_k - F_{y,k}^{des}\cos\psi_k}{T_k^{des}}\right), \quad
\theta_k^{des} = \arctan2\left(F_{x,k}^{des}\cos\psi_k + F_{y,k}^{des}\sin\psi_k, F_{z,k}^{des}\right). \tag{2.4}
$$

*Assuming the inner-loop high-frequency attitude controller ensures the actual Euler angles* $\eta_k$ *converge rapidly within* $T_{att} \ll \Delta t$ *such that the attitude tracking error is bounded by* $\|\eta_k - \eta_k^{des}\|_2 \le e_{\eta}$ *. By the local Lipschitz continuity of the 3D rotation matrix* $R(\eta)$ *with Lipschitz constant* $L_R$ *, the acceleration-level perturbation induced by attitude tracking lag satisfies:*

$$
\|a_{\eta,k}\|_2 = \left\|\frac{u_k^{act}-u_k^{des}}{m}\right\|_2 \le \frac{1}{m} T_{max} L_R e_{\eta} \triangleq \delta_d^a. \tag{2.5}
$$

*Consequently, the cascaded inner-loop tracking lag can be treated as a bounded additive state perturbation* $d_k = [0_{3}^{T},\, \Delta t\, a_{\eta,k}^{T}]^T$ *in (2.2), with* $\|d_k\|_2 \le \Delta t\,\delta_d^a \triangleq \delta_d$ *, thereby preserving the 6D translational NMPC abstraction and defining the state-level disturbance bound used later in the stability analysis.*

### C. Nominal NMPC Formulation

At each sampling instant $k$, the nominal NMPC expert solver solves the following finite-horizon non-convex parameterized optimization problem $\mathcal{P}(x_k)$:

$$
\min_{\mathbf{U}_k} J(x_k, \mathbf{U}_k) = \sum_{i=0}^{H-1} \left( \|x_{k+i|k} - x_{sp}\|_{Q_x}^2 + \|u_{k+i|k}\|_{R_u}^2 + \Psi_{obs}(p_{k+i|k}) \right) + \|x_{k+H|k} - x_{sp}\|_{P_f}^2 \tag{2.6}
$$

where the soft obstacle potential used by the expert and the $Q$-head rollout-cost label is

$$
\Psi_{obs}(p) = \rho_{obs}\sum_{j=1}^{M}\left[\max\left(0, r_{obs,j}+\delta_{soft}-\|p-p_{obs,j}\|_2\right)\right]^2 . \tag{2.6a}
$$
 
$$
\text{s.t. } x_{k+i+1|k} = g_0(x_{k+i|k}, u_{k+i|k}), \quad \forall i = 0, \dots, H-1 \tag{2.7}
$$
 
$$
x_{k+i|k} \in \mathcal{X}, \quad u_{k+i|k} \in \mathcal{U}, \quad \forall i = 0, \dots, H-1 \tag{2.8}
$$
 
$$
x_{k+H|k} \in \mathcal{X}_f \quad \text{(optional; in the released script, } \mathcal{X}_f=\mathcal{X} \text{ and terminal shaping is imposed by } P_f\text{).} \tag{2.9}
$$

Here $g_0(\cdot)$ is the nominal gravity-precompensated discrete-time translational dynamics, $Q_x \succeq 0$, $R_u \succ 0$, and $P_f \succ 0$ are weighting matrices, and $\rho_{obs}>0$ and $\delta_{soft}\ge0$ specify the soft non-convex obstacle potential. The CBF layer in Section 4 enforces a hard safety filter, while $\Psi_{obs}$ makes the expert and the $Q$-head cost label sensitive to obstacle geometry. The optimized sequence is $\mathbf{U}_k = [u_{k|k}^T, \dots, u_{k+H-1|k}^T]^T \in \mathbb{R}^{3H}$. Since the obstacle field is non-convex, the notation $\mathbf{U}_k^* = \kappa_{nmpc}(x_k)$ denotes the best sequence returned by the numerical expert solver used for data generation, not a certified global optimum.

According to the receding-horizon principle, the physical outer-loop command applied at time $k$ is the first element of the optimized sequence: $u_k = [\kappa_{nmpc}(x_k)]_0$.

**Remark 1 (Hierarchical Control Architecture).** *In standard quadrotor control design, the 12D full-state dynamics (2.1) are typically decoupled into a high-frequency inner-loop attitude tracking controller (governing Euler angles* $\eta_k$ *and angular rates* $\omega_k$ *) and a lower-frequency outer-loop translational guidance controller. Under this cascade structure, the inner loop is assumed to track the commanded thrust and attitude orientation with negligible delay, allowing the outer-loop guidance controller (such as our proposed NMPC) to operate on the 6-dimensional translational dynamics with the virtual control input representing the desired gravity-precompensated force command. This cascade assumption is widely adopted in quadrotor navigation literature and is the exact model simulated in Section 6.*

## 3\. Latent Space Recursive Control Law Approximation (TRM-NMPC Framework)

This section details the mapping of the numerical solver's online iterative optimization steps into the multi-step latent space recursive evolution of a tiny network \[13\].

### A. Latent-Space Recursive Dynamics

Define the network input feature vector $X_k = [x_k^T, x_{sp}^T]^T \in \mathbb{R}^{12}$, where $x_k = [p_k^T, v_k^T]^T \in \mathbb{R}^6$ is the outer-loop translational state and $x_{sp}\in\mathbb{R}^6$ is the stationary setpoint. This 12-dimensional feature vector is distinct from the physical 12D quadrotor state $x_{12d,k}$ in Section 2. Let $z_t\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$ denote the recursive latent state and $y_t\in\mathbb{R}^{d}$ denote the decoded-sequence latent state, with $d=64$ and $3H=30$.

The implemented PyTorch model uses weight sharing across recursive steps and two LayerNorm--Tanh--Linear cells:

$$
\xi_t^{z}= (W_x X_k+b_x) + (W_y \hat{\mathbf{U}}_{t-1}+b_y) + (W_z z_{t-1}+b_z), \quad
z_t = \tanh\!\left(W_{cz}\tanh\!\left(\mathrm{LN}_z(\xi_t^{z})\right)+b_{cz}\right), \quad t = 1, \dots, D . \tag{3.1}
$$
 
$$
\xi_t^{y}= (M_y y_{t-1}+b_{My})+(M_z z_t+b_{Mz}), \quad
y_t = \tanh\!\left(W_{cy}\tanh\!\left(\mathrm{LN}_y(\xi_t^{y})\right)+b_{cy}\right). \tag{3.2}
$$
 
$$
\hat{\mathbf{U}}_{t} = f_O(y_t). \tag{3.3}
$$

Here $W_x\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times 12}$, $W_y\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times 3H}$, $W_z,M_y,M_z\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$, $W_{cz},W_{cy}\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$, and $f_O:\mathbb{R}^{d}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^{3H}$ are trainable but shared across recursion depth. The initial states are $z_0=0$, $y_0=0$, and $\hat{\mathbf{U}}_0=0$; the final predicted sequence is $\hat{\mathbf{U}}_D$ with $D=16$.

### C. Deep Supervision & Computational Complexity Analysis

During the offline training phase, the dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{(X_k, \mathbf{U}_k^*)\}$ is generated by the nominal numerical solver. To encourage the latent trajectories to approach the numerical expert sequence over recursion depth, we apply deep supervision across all recursive steps with a discount factor $\gamma \in (0,1]$ (set to $\gamma = 0.95$):

$$
\mathcal{L}_{policy}(\theta) = \sum_{(X_k, \mathbf{U}_k^*) \in \mathcal{D}} \sum_{t=1}^{D} \gamma^{D-t} \| f_O(y_t) - \mathbf{U}_k^* \|_2^2 \tag{3.4}
$$

where higher weights are assigned to later steps to guide the final decoded sequence toward the numerical expert solution returned by the solver.

## 4\. Latent Space Stochastic Exploration & Verification (Test-Time Scaling)

Deterministic approximate controllers are prone to failing in complex, non-convex landscapes because they lack online self-correction. For instance, when a quadrotor encounters OOD aerodynamic disturbances, a single-pass network might output an action that traps the vehicle in a local minimum directly in front of an obstacle.

To address this, we introduce **Test-Time Compute Scaling** to explore the non-convex landscape online.

```markdown
[Latent Noise Injection]
                          │
      ┌───────────────────┼───────────────────┐ (Parallel Exploration K)
      ▼                   ▼                   ▼
 [Branch 1]          [Branch 2]          [Branch K]
      │                   │                   │
      ▼                   ▼                   ▼
(Candidate 1)       (Candidate 2)       (Candidate K)
      │                   │                   │
      └───────────────────┼───────────────────┘
                          ▼
                [Regression Q-Head]
                          │
                          ▼
              (Optimal Action Selected)
```

### A. Latent Space Clamped Gaussian Noise Injection & Attractor Escape

At each recursive step $t = 1,\dots,D$ during online inference, we inject bounded clamped Gaussian noise across $K$ parallel branches:

$$
\tilde\epsilon_t^{(\ell)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2 I_d), \quad
\epsilon_t^{(\ell)} = \operatorname{clip}(\tilde\epsilon_t^{(\ell)},-\Delta_z,\Delta_z), \quad
z_{t-1}^{(\ell)} \leftarrow z_{t-1}^{(\ell)} + \epsilon_t^{(\ell)}, \quad \forall \ell=1,\dots,K . \tag{4.1}
$$

where $\Delta_z > 0$ restricts the element-wise noise magnitude. This matches the implementation, which samples Gaussian noise and then clamps each latent component rather than drawing from an exact truncated-normal sampler.

#### Mathematical Mechanism: Metastable Attractor Escape via Freidlin-Wentzell Large Deviation Theory

The recursive network equations (3.1) and (3.2) form a discrete-time deterministic dynamical system, which under noise perturbation can be modeled as a non-linear stochastic mapping: $z_t = \Phi_\theta(z_{t-1}, X_k) + \epsilon_t$. In the absence of noise, this mapping exhibits several metastable attractors, some of which correspond to local minima of the non-convex optimization problem (bad basins).

According to the **Freidlin-Wentzell discrete-time Large Deviation Theory (LDT)**, we define the local rate function $L(z, z')$ of the transition step as:

$$
L(z, z') = \frac{1}{2} \| z' - \Phi_\theta(z, X_k) \|_2^2 \tag{4.2}
$$

For a potential transition path of length $D$, defined as $\phi = \{z_0, z_1, \dots, z_D\}$, its action functional $I(\phi)$ is mathematically formulated as:

$$
I(\phi) = \sum_{t=1}^{D} L(z_{t-1}, z_t) \tag{4.3}
$$

The transition of the latent trajectory from a suboptimal attractor basin $G$ toward a lower-cost basin can be interpreted through a quasi-potential barrier $\Delta U$. The quasi-potential is defined as the infimum of the action functional $I(\phi)$ over all feasible transition paths starting at the local attractor $z_{\text{att}}$ and reaching the boundary $\partial G$:

$$
\Delta U = \inf \{ I(\phi) \mid \phi = \{z_0, \dots, z_D\}, z_0 = z_{\text{att}}, z_D \in \partial G\} \tag{4.4}
$$

The Mean First Passage Time (MFPT) for the trajectory to escape the bad basin $G$ satisfies the following exponential relation:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\tau] \asymp \exp\left(\frac{2 \Delta U}{\sigma^2}\right) \tag{4.5}
$$

Under the simplified assumption that each stochastic rollout has independent single-chain escape probability $p_e$, the probability that at least one rollout escapes the local basin within $D$ steps is:

$$
P_{escape} = 1 - \left( 1 - p_e \right)^K \tag{4.6}
$$

where $p_e \approx 1 - \exp(-D/\mathbb{E}[\tau])$ is a heuristic finite-depth escape probability for a single chain. This expression is used as a motivation for width scaling, not as a closed-loop success guarantee.

**Remark 3 (Applicability of LDT to Non-Zero Noise and Finite Horizon):** *Since the injected noise intensity* $\sigma$ *in practical real-time implementations is a non-zero constant (e.g.,* $\sigma = 0.25$ *or* $0.3$ *in Section 6) and the latent recursive depth is finite (* $D = 16$ *), the mathematical formulations of Freidlin-Wentzell LDT are utilized as an LDT-inspired qualitative catalyst to explain the latent state transition dynamics and motivate the parallel scaling search, rather than a strict quantitative bound under the asymptotic weak noise limit (* $\sigma \to 0$ *) and infinite horizon (* $t \to \infty$ *). Transient command perturbations are filtered by the deterministic projection operator (4.15), while the resulting residual state perturbations are treated in the practical safety and robust-stability analysis of Theorems 1 and 2. Quantitatively, the quasi-potential barrier* $\Delta U$ *serves to explain how the accumulation of the neural network's latent transition errors acts as a physical barrier. A higher* $\Delta U$ *indicates a larger topological barrier to escape a sub-optimal basin, motivating wider parallel widths* $K$ *to improve the chance of escape.*

### B. Value-Aligned Native Regression Q-Head and Hysteresis Stabilization

To select the optimal sequence among the $K$ candidates, we co-train a lightweight $Q$-head regression network $f_Q$ parameterized by $\phi$. The $Q$-head is trained to fit the actual NMPC trajectory cost of the decoded control sequence. The joint loss function is:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{Q}(\phi) = \sum_{(X_k, \mathbf{U}_k^*) \in \mathcal{D}} \left\| f_Q(y_D) - \left[ V_{\max} - J(x_k, f_O(y_D)) \right] \right\|_2^2 \tag{4.7}
$$

where $J(x_k, f_O(y_D))$ is the rollout trajectory cost computed by forward-integrating the nominal dynamics under the currently decoded sequence $f_O(y_D)$ using the same stage cost, terminal cost, input cost, and soft obstacle terms in (2.6)--(2.6a), and $V_{\max}=150.0$ is a constant offset used only to convert lower rollout costs into larger regression targets. The implementation does not clip $V_{\max}-J$, so the $Q$-head preserves relative ordering even among high-cost candidates. A higher $Q$-score indicates a lower-cost trajectory under the nominal rollout model. The released script optimizes the joint objective $\mathcal{L}_{total}=\mathcal{L}_{policy}+\lambda_Q\mathcal{L}_{Q}$ with $\lambda_Q=0.1$, so the evaluator is trained jointly but does not override the supervised policy-matching loss.

To prevent high-frequency chattering or jumping between different homotopy branches during receding-horizon execution (which may break average dwell-time constraints in switched systems), we incorporate a trajectory-space hysteresis penalty into the online selection rule. Let $\hat{\mathbf{U}}_{shift,k-1} \triangleq [\hat u_{1|k-1}^{T}, \dots, \hat u_{H-1|k-1}^{T}, \hat u_{H-1|k-1}^{T}]^T \in \mathbb{R}^{3H}$ represent the shifted decoded sequence selected at the previous time step before first-command safety filtering. The online selection is then performed via:

$$
\ell^* = \arg\max_{\ell \in \{1,\dots,K\}} \left( f_Q\left(y_D^{(\ell)}\right) - \eta_{\text{hyst}} \left\| \hat{\mathbf{U}}_D^{(\ell)} - \hat{\mathbf{U}}_{shift,k-1} \right\|_2^2 \right) \tag{4.8}
$$

where $\hat{\mathbf{U}}_D^{(\ell)} = f_O\left(y_D^{(\ell)}\right) \in \mathbb{R}^{3H}$ is the candidate control sequence generated by the $\ell$-th sample branch, and $\eta_{\text{hyst}} \ge 0$ is the trajectory-level hysteresis regularization parameter. This penalty restricts frequent switches between different homotopy branches, stabilizing the closed-loop receding-horizon sequence.

**Remark 4 (Statistical Justification of Generalization Bound):** *The generalization error bound* $\epsilon_Q$ *can be formally quantified using Rademacher Complexity and PAC-Bayes generalization bounds in statistical learning theory. Under a compact decision domain* $\mathcal{U}^H$ *and Lipschitz stage costs, the generalization gap shrinks at a rate of* $\epsilon_Q \propto \mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{N_{\text{samples}}})$ *where* $N_{\text{samples}}$ *is the cardinality of the offline generated dataset* $\mathcal{D}$ *. This motivates a statistical connection between dataset size and the expected suboptimality of the learned evaluator, although the actual constant depends on the hypothesis class and data distribution.*

**Remark 5 (Out-of-Distribution Safety Fallbacks):** *When the quadrotor operates in extreme OOD regions (e.g., under unmodeled wind gusts or severe physical parameter mismatches), the local generalization error* $\epsilon_Q$ *of the co-trained* $Q$-head may increase locally. Under such adversarial conditions, the PTRM-NMPC framework provides a dual-layer safety fallback mechanism. First, the latent-space stochastic exploration (Width Scaling) continues to generate highly diverse paths, which increases the probability that at least one rollout has a lower-cost feasible geometry. Second, the bottom-level convex projection layer (4.15) is mathematically decoupled from both the recursive neural networks and the $Q$-head evaluator. It acts directly on the current physical states ($p_k, v_k$), so even if the $Q$-head selects a sub-optimal trajectory, the CBF projection (4.15) filters the first physical command subject to feasibility and linearization residuals. This separates neural approximation errors from the final safety-filtered command, rather than eliminating all physical risk unconditionally.*

### C. Discrete-Time Composite Control Barrier Function (DT-CCBF)

To avoid discrete-continuous mismatch, we formulate a **Discrete-Time Composite Control Barrier Function (DT-CCBF)** aligned with the explicit translational discretization used in the script. For $M$ spherical obstacles located at $p_{obs,j}$ with radii $r_{obs,j}$, the non-convex state safety boundaries are defined as:

$$
h_j(p_k) = \|p_k - p_{obs,j}\|_2^2 - (r_{obs,j} + \delta_{buffer})^2 \ge 0 \tag{4.9}
$$

where $\delta_{buffer} > 0$ is a conservative safety buffer added to the obstacle radii to increase the physical clearance margin during transient safety index degradation. Since the system has a relative degree of $2$ from position to control force, we construct a discrete-time first-order auxiliary barrier function for each obstacle:

$$
B_j(p_k, v_k) = h_j(p_{k+1}) - (1 - \alpha_d) h_j(p_k) \ge 0 \tag{4.10}
$$

where $\alpha_d \in (0, 1]$ (set to $\alpha_d = 0.8$) is a contractive tuning parameter. To handle multiple obstacles simultaneously without non-smoothness chattering, we merge individual barriers into a smooth minimum barrier utilizing the Log-Sum-Exponential (LSE) operator:

$$
B_{smooth}(p_k, v_k) = -\frac{1}{\kappa} \ln \left( \sum_{j=1}^M \exp\left(-\kappa B_j(p_k, v_k)\right) \right) \tag{4.11}
$$

where $\kappa = 5.0$. The nominal discrete safety condition is defined through the following dissipation inequality:

$$
B_{smooth}(p_{k+1}, v_{k+1}) - B_{smooth}(p_k, v_k) \ge -\gamma_d B_{smooth}(p_k, v_k) \tag{4.12}
$$

where $\gamma_d \in (0, 1]$ (set to $\gamma_d = 0.2$) is the safety decrease parameter. Under the gravity-precompensated nominal translation dynamics (2.3), the future states propagate as:

$$
p_{k+1} = p_k + \Delta t v_k \tag{4.12a}
$$
 
$$
v_{k+1} = v_k + \Delta t \left( \frac{u_k}{m} - \frac{K_d}{m} v_k \right) \tag{4.12b}
$$

Since position $p_k$ has relative degree two with respect to the virtual control force $u_k$, the control input does not explicitly appear in $p_{k+1}$, but rather in $p_{k+2}$ as follows:

$$
p_{k+2} = p_{k+1} + \Delta t v_{k+1} = p_{k+2, drift} + \frac{\Delta t^2}{m} u_k \tag{4.12c}
$$

where $p_{k+2, drift}$ denotes the nominal uncontrolled (drift) position at step $k+2$ under $u_k = \mathbf{0}$:

$$
p_{k+2, drift} \triangleq p_k + 2\Delta t v_k - \Delta t^2 \frac{K_d}{m} v_k \tag{4.12d}
$$

Thus, substituting the state predictions into the contractive safety dissipation inequality (4.12) and performing a first-order Taylor expansion around the uncontrolled drift sequence ($u_k = \mathbf{0}$) yields a linear inequality constraint with respect to the control input $u_k$:

$$
A_{smooth}^T u_k \le b_{smooth} \tag{4.13}
$$

where the control coefficient matrix $A_{smooth} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is the first-order relative-degree-two safety gradient evaluated at the uncontrolled drift point:

$$
A_{smooth} = \sum_{j=1}^M w_j \left( -\frac{2\Delta t^2}{m} \left(p_{k+2, drift} - p_{obs,j}\right) \right), \quad w_j = \frac{\exp(-\kappa B_{j, drift})}{\sum_{i=1}^M \exp(-\kappa B_{i, drift})} \tag{4.14}
$$

with $B_{j, drift} \triangleq h_j(p_{k+2, drift}) - (1 - \alpha_d) h_j(p_{k+1})$ evaluated along the nominal uncontrolled drift trajectory, and the scalar boundary limit $b_{smooth} \in \mathbb{R}$ is defined as:

$$
b_{smooth} = B_{smooth, drift} - (1 - \gamma_d) B_{smooth}(p_k, v_k) \tag{4.14a}
$$

with $B_{smooth, drift}$ computed from $B_{j,drift}$ at $p_{k+2,drift}$ and $p_{k+1}$ according to (4.10)--(4.11).

Because (4.13) is obtained from a first-order Taylor approximation of the smooth barrier around the drift trajectory, the true nonlinear barrier inequality is satisfied up to a bounded local residual $\epsilon_{lin}$ on the compact operating set. The admissible linearized safe control set is then represented by the half-space $\mathcal{U}_{ad}(x_k) = \{ u \in \mathcal{U} \mid A_{smooth}^T u \le b_{smooth} \}$. At each control step, the nominal control action $u_{k, nominal}^* = [\hat{\mathbf{U}}_{D}^{(\ell^*)}]_{0:3}$ selected by the $Q$-head is projected onto this set via the following convex Euclidean projection. Because the implemented constraint consists of a box and one linearized half-space, the released script solves this projection using a Lagrange-multiplier bisection rather than a generic QP solver:

$$
u_k^{safe} = \mathcal{P}_{\mathcal{U}_{ad}(x_k)}\left(u_{k, nominal}^*\right) = \arg\min_{u \in \mathcal{U}_{ad}(x_k) \cap \mathcal{U}} \left\|u - u_{k, nominal}^*\right\|_2^2 \tag{4.15}
$$

**Conservatism and Tuning of Composite CBF.** *The parameter* $\alpha_d$ *plays a crucial role in regulating the conservatism of the safety boundary. Physically,* $\alpha_d$ *represents a contractive limit on the maximum allowable rate of approach towards the obstacles. A smaller value of* $\alpha_d$ *translates to more conservative flight behavior, forcing the quadrotor to decelerate early and maintain a larger safe distance. Conversely, a larger* $\alpha_d$ *grants the quadrotor more agile maneuvering but increases the risk of actuation saturation near the boundary. This represents an explicit engineering trade-off between the safety margin and system agility.*

**Assumption 1 (Actuator Saturation and MCIS Compatibility):** *In practical control engineering, the barrier construction* $h(x)$ *must be designed compatible with actuator saturation limits* $\mathcal{U}$ *. To avoid feasibility failures under saturation, the safe set* $\mathcal{C}$ *must be designed as a compact subset of the system's Maximal Control Invariant Set (MCIS), i.e., restricting* $\mathcal{C} \subset \text{MCIS}(\mathcal{U})$ *is used as a design condition intended to keep the admissible safe control set* $\mathcal{U}_{ad}(x_k) \cap \mathcal{U}$ *non-empty in the nominal operating envelope.*

### D. Actuator Saturation and Discontinuous Fallback Safety Policy

Under extreme circumstances (e.g., large wind gusts or high velocities near obstacles), the physical bounds of the actuators may conflict with the safety conditions, resulting in an empty intersection of the admissible and physical control sets:

$$
\mathcal{U}_{ad}(x_k) \cap \mathcal{U} = \emptyset
$$

This makes the projection problem in (4.15) infeasible. To preserve physical input feasibility and reduce safety-index degradation under saturation, we introduce a **Discontinuous Fallback Policy** $\kappa_b(x_k)$:

$$
\kappa_b(x_k) = \arg\min_{u \in \mathcal{U}} A_{smooth}^T u \tag{4.16}
$$

When QP infeasibility is detected, this policy is triggered: the system abandons tracking the nominal trajectory and applies the component-wise box-constrained input that minimizes $A_{smooth}^T u$ exactly:

$$
[u_k^{safe}]_i =
\begin{cases}
u_{min}, & [A_{smooth}]_i \ge 0,\\
u_{max}, & [A_{smooth}]_i < 0,
\end{cases}
\quad i=1,2,3. \tag{4.17}
$$

This fallback policy preserves input feasibility under saturation and applies the box-constrained command that most reduces the linearized barrier violation; the remaining safety loss is covered by the practical residual bound in Theorem 1.

## 5\. Theoretical Analysis: Practical Safety & Practical Stability

This section presents practical safety and robust-stability arguments for the closed-loop system under persistent perturbations. Hard recursive feasibility is replaced by explicit feasibility assumptions and by the fallback mechanism when the linearized safe set becomes empty.

### A. Closed-Loop Practical Safety and Self-Healing Analysis

**Assumption 2 (Safety Set Invariance):** *Let the nominal safe state set be defined as* $\mathcal{C} = \{(p, v) \mid B_{smooth}(p, v) \ge 0\}$ *. We assume* $\mathcal{C}$ *is a compact subset of the Maximal Control Invariant Set (MCIS), and there always exists a physical control input that can maintain the state within* $\mathcal{C}$ *when no extreme external disturbances are present.*

**Theorem 1 (Practical Safety and Self-Healing).** *Let the initial state satisfy* $x_0 \in \mathcal{C}$ *. Under the joint action of the physical actuator saturation constraints and the discontinuous fallback policy (4.16), the control input* $u_k^{safe}$ *is physically feasible at every step (* $u_k^{safe} \in \mathcal{U}$ *), and the closed-loop state trajectory maintains practical invariance. That is, the system can recover toward* $\mathcal{C}$ *in finite steps after minor safety violations under saturation.*

**Proof.** We prove this via mathematical induction.

1. **Base Case:** At $k=0$, the initial state satisfies $x_0 \in \mathcal{C} \implies B_{smooth}(p_0, v_0) \ge 0$.
2. **Inductive Step:** Assume that at step $k$, $x_k \in \mathcal{C}$ holds. We analyze the feasibility and evolution of the control action:
	- **Case A:** If the admissible and physical control sets have a non-empty intersection, i.e., $\mathcal{U}_{ad}(x_k) \cap \mathcal{U} \neq \emptyset$. The convex projection (4.15) has a unique solution $u_k^{safe}$ satisfying:
		$$
		A_{smooth}^T u_k^{safe} \le b_{smooth} \tag{5.1}
		$$
		By the discrete CBF design (4.12) and the bounded linearization residual $\epsilon_{lin}$, this yields the practical bound:
		$$
		B_{smooth}(p_{k+1}, v_{k+1}) - B_{smooth}(p_k, v_k) \ge -\gamma_d B_{smooth}(p_k, v_k)-\epsilon_{lin} \tag{5.2}
		$$
		 
		$$
		B_{smooth}(p_{k+1}, v_{k+1}) \ge (1 - \gamma_d) B_{smooth}(p_k, v_k)-\epsilon_{lin}. \tag{5.3}
		$$
		Hence, $x_{k+1}$ remains in a practical neighborhood of $\mathcal{C}$; exact invariance requires $\epsilon_{lin}=0$ or sufficient barrier margin.
		- **Case B:** If extreme actuator saturation yields $\mathcal{U}_{ad}(x_k) \cap \mathcal{U} = \emptyset$, the projection problem becomes infeasible. The system triggers the fallback policy (4.16) to apply the box-constrained input $\kappa_b(x_k) \in \mathcal{U}$ that minimizes the linearized barrier-growth term $A_{smooth}^T u$. In this case, the dissipation inequality (5.2) may be temporarily violated, allowing the state to briefly exit the nominal safe set:
		$$
		B_{smooth}(p_{k+1}, v_{k+1}) < 0 \tag{5.4}
		$$
		However, due to the gradient-descent nature of $\kappa_b(x_k)$, the first-order violation rate is bounded by the physical input limits and the residual terms:
		$$
		B_{smooth}(p_{k+1}, v_{k+1}) - B_{smooth}(p_k, v_k) \ge \min_{u \in \mathcal{U}} A_{smooth}^T u - \phi(x_k) \triangleq -\epsilon_{sat} \tag{5.5}
		$$
		In obstacle-approach cases this command acts as an emergency repulsive or braking input relative to the active linearized safety gradient. Crucially, since the safe invariant manifold $\mathcal{C}$ is synthesized with a built-in conservative safety margin (e.g., using an inflated obstacle radius $r_{obs,j} + \delta_{\text{buffer}}$ in (4.9)), the physical distance to any obstacle is intended to remain positive if the buffer dominates the accumulated residual and disturbance terms, thereby reducing collision risk despite the index degradation ($B_{smooth} < 0$). As the velocity decreases, the admissible control set is reconstructed, under the MCIS-compatibility and bounded-disturbance assumptions, allowing that in finite steps $N_{heal}$, we recover $\mathcal{U}_{ad}(x_{k+N_{heal}}) \cap \mathcal{U} \neq \emptyset$, and the system is designed to recover to the nominal safe set $\mathcal{C}$ (i.e., $B_{smooth}(x_{k+N_{heal}+1}) \ge 0$).

Therefore, the closed-loop system maintains practical safety and self-healing under actuator saturation within the stated feasibility, buffer, and bounded-residual assumptions. $\blacksquare$

### B. Practical Input-to-State Stability under Multi-Homotopy Switching

In non-convex obstacle fields, the selected nominal NMPC control law $\kappa_{nmpc}(x_k)$ can jump abruptly between different homotopy paths (e.g., bypassing an obstacle on the left vs. right). This optimal path bifurcation causes the optimal value function $J^*(x_k)$ to be discontinuous across the bifurcation boundaries. To analyze stability under these discontinuities, we model the system as a switched system. Throughout this subsection, stability is stated with respect to the tracking error $\tilde{x}_k \triangleq x_k-x_{sp}$, while $V_\sigma(x_k)$ denotes the NMPC value function parameterized by the fixed target $x_{sp}$.

**Assumption 3 (Partitioned Manifold and Local Continuity):** *The safe set* $\mathcal{C}$ *can be partitioned into* $N_c$ *compact cells:* $\mathcal{C} = \bigcup_{j=1}^{N_c} \mathcal{C}_j$ *. Within each cell, the optimal value function* $V_{\sigma}(x) = J^{\sigma}(x)$ *is locally Lipschitz continuous with a Lipschitz constant* $L_v$, *where* $\sigma \in \{1,\dots,N_c\}$ *is the switching signal indexing the active cell. When the state crosses the bifurcation boundary* $\mathcal{M} = \bigcup_{i \neq j} (\partial \mathcal{C}_i \cap \partial \mathcal{C}_j)$ *due to stochastic exploration, the jump in the value function is bounded by a constant* $\epsilon_d > 0$:

$$
V_{\sigma(k_{j})}(x_{k_{j}}) \le \mu V_{\sigma(k_{j}-1)}(x_{k_{j}}) \tag{5.6}
$$

*where the jump multiplier satisfies* $\mu = 1 + \frac{\epsilon_d}{V_{min}} \ge 1$ *with* $V_{min} > 0$ *as the lower bound of the value function on the boundary.*

**Theorem 2 (Practical ISS under Switching).** *Let the closed-loop total perturbation bound be* $e_{total} \triangleq \delta(K, \sigma, \Delta_z, \epsilon_Q) + \delta_w + \delta_d$ *, where* $\delta(K, \sigma, \Delta_z, \epsilon_Q)$ *is the combined neural approximation and selection error. Assume the switching signal* $\sigma(k)$ *satisfies the Average Dwell-Time (ADT) constraint:*

$$
N_{\sigma}(k_0, k) \le N_0 + \frac{k - k_0}{\tau_a} \tag{5.7}
$$

*where the average dwell-time satisfies:*

$$
\tau_a > \tau_a^* = \frac{\ln \mu}{\ln(1 / (1 - \lambda))} \tag{5.8}
$$

*with* $\lambda \in (0, 1)$ *as the decay parameter of the nominal Lyapunov function. Then, the closed-loop system under PTRM-NMPC is practically Input-to-State Stable (pISS), and the state trajectory asymptotically converges to the residual set* $\mathcal{B}_p$:

$$
\mathcal{B}_p = \left\{ x \in \mathcal{C} \, \middle| \, \|x-x_{sp}\|_2 \le \alpha_1^{-1}\left( \frac{\mu^{N_0} \beta(e_{total})}{\bar{\lambda}} \right) \right\} \tag{5.9}
$$

*where* $\bar{\lambda} = 1 - (1-\lambda)\mu^{1/\tau_a} > 0$ *represents the effective decay rate of the switched system under the ADT constraint.*

**Assumption 4 (Lipschitz Continuity of Stage Cost and System Dynamics):** *The stage cost* $L(x, u)$ *and the system dynamics* $g(x, u)$ *are Lipschitz continuous on the compact set* $\mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{U}$ *.*

**Assumption 5 (Bounded Slope of Nominal NMPC Value Function):** *The nominal NMPC optimal value function $J^* (x)$ is locally Lipschitz continuous in the safe set $\mathcal{C}$, i.e., there exists a Lipschitz constant $L_v > 0$ such that for any $x_1, x_2 \in \mathcal{C}$, the inequality $|J^*(x_1) - J^*(x_2)| \le L_v \|x_1 - x_2\|_2$ holds. Since the safe set $\mathcal{C}$ is restricted to a compact interior subset of $\mathcal{X}$, boundary singularities and abrupt constraint-switching points are avoided, supporting the use of a local Lipschitz constant under the stated regularity assumptions.*

To establish suboptimality bounds for the native $Q$-head selection, we state and prove the following lemma:

**Lemma 2 (Suboptimality Bound of Native $Q$-head Selection).** *Let the co-trained* $Q$-head $f_Q$ *have a bounded generalization error* $\epsilon_Q > 0$ *on the compact candidate-control domain, satisfying:*

$$
\max_{\ell \in \{1,\dots,K\}} \left| f_Q(y_D^{(\ell)}) - \left[V_{\max} - J\left(x_k, \hat{\mathbf{U}}_D^{(\ell)}\right)\right] \right| \le \epsilon_Q. \tag{5.10}
$$

*If the hysteresis term in (4.8) is set to zero or treated separately as a bounded regularization term, the selected sequence* $\hat{\mathbf{U}}_D^{(\ell^*)}$ *satisfies the following suboptimality bound with respect to the absolute minimum cost among the $K$ candidates:*

$$
J\left(x_k, \hat{\mathbf{U}}_D^{(\ell^*)}\right) \le \min_{\ell \in \{1,\dots,K\}} J\left(x_k, \hat{\mathbf{U}}_D^{(\ell)}\right) + 2\epsilon_Q. \tag{5.11}
$$

**Proof.** From the selection rule (4.8) without the hysteresis regularizer, $f_Q(y_D^{(\ell^*)}) \ge f_Q(y_D^{(\ell)})$ holds for all $\ell \in \{1, \dots, K\}$. Applying the bounded error assumption (5.10), we expand the inequality:

$$
V_{\max} - J\left(x_k, \hat{\mathbf{U}}_D^{(\ell^*)}\right) + \epsilon_Q \ge f_Q(y_D^{(\ell^*)}) \ge f_Q(y_D^{(\ell)}) \ge V_{\max} - J\left(x_k, \hat{\mathbf{U}}_D^{(\ell)}\right) - \epsilon_Q.
$$

Subtracting the constant $V_{\max}$ and rearranging terms yields:

$$
J\left(x_k, \hat{\mathbf{U}}_D^{(\ell^*)}\right) \le J\left(x_k, \hat{\mathbf{U}}_D^{(\ell)}\right) + 2\epsilon_Q.
$$

Since this inequality holds for any candidate index $\ell \in \{1, \dots, K\}$, it must also hold for the candidate that minimizes the cost. This completes the proof. $\blacksquare$

We now prove that the convex projection onto the safe set does not expand the control approximation error:

**Lemma 3 (Non-expansiveness of Safe Convex Projection).** *Let the safe input set* $S \triangleq \mathcal{U}_{ad}(x_k) \cap \mathcal{U}$ *be a convex closed set. For any control vectors* $u_1, u_2 \in \mathbb{R}^3$, *the convex projection operator* $\mathcal{P}_S(\cdot)$ *is non-expansive, with its Lipschitz constant bounded by 1, i.e.,:*

$$
\| \mathcal{P}_S(u_1) - \mathcal{P}_S(u_2) \|_2 \le \| u_1 - u_2 \|_2 \tag{5.12}
$$

**Proof.** Based on the projection theorem in Hilbert spaces, the projection onto the closed convex set $S$ satisfies the variational inequality: $\langle u_i - \mathcal{P}_S(u_i), v - \mathcal{P}_S(u_i) \rangle \le 0, \forall v \in S$. Substituting $v = \mathcal{P}_S(u_2)$ for $u_1$ and $v = \mathcal{P}_S(u_1)$ for $u_2$, and summing the two resulting inequalities:

$$
\langle (u_1 - u_2) - (\mathcal{P}_S(u_1) - \mathcal{P}_S(u_2)), \mathcal{P}_S(u_1) - \mathcal{P}_S(u_2) \rangle \ge 0
$$

Applying the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, we obtain:

$$
\| \mathcal{P}_S(u_1) - \mathcal{P}_S(u_2) \|_2^2 \le \langle u_1 - u_2, \mathcal{P}_S(u_1) - \mathcal{P}_S(u_2) \rangle \le \| u_1 - u_2 \|_2 \cdot \| \mathcal{P}_S(u_1) - \mathcal{P}_S(u_2) \|_2
$$

Dividing both sides by the non-zero norm, we prove the non-expansiveness property (5.12). This completes the proof. $\blacksquare$

On feasible projection steps, the appropriate reference is the safe-filtered numerical expert command $u_k^{ref} \triangleq \mathcal{P}_S([\kappa_{nmpc}(x_k)]_0)$, because the numerical expert in (2.6) contains a soft obstacle potential while the CBF layer imposes the hard safety filter. The non-expansiveness property then gives

$$
\|u_k^{safe} - u_k^{ref}\|_2 = \| \mathcal{P}_S(u_{k, nominal}^*) - \mathcal{P}_S([\kappa_{nmpc}(x_k)]_0) \|_2 \le \| u_{k, nominal}^* - [\kappa_{nmpc}(x_k)]_0 \|_2 . \tag{5.13}
$$

Thus, during feasible projection steps, the Euclidean projection layer does not amplify command-space approximation error relative to the same safety filter applied to the expert. If the fallback policy (4.16) is triggered, the projection non-expansiveness argument no longer applies; the resulting bounded fallback deviation is instead absorbed into the residual term used in the practical safety and pISS bounds. Combining Lemma 2, the clamping limit $\Delta_z$, and this bounded fallback residual yields the compact-domain bound used in the stability proof:

$$
\|e_k\|_2 \le \delta_{app}+\delta_{sel}(K,\sigma,\Delta_z)+c_Q\epsilon_Q+\delta_{fb} \triangleq \delta(K, \sigma, \Delta_z, \epsilon_Q), \tag{5.14}
$$

where $\delta_{app}$ is the supervised policy-approximation floor, $\delta_{sel}$ is the candidate-selection gap induced by finite-width stochastic sampling, $c_Q\epsilon_Q$ is the evaluator-ranking contribution, and $\delta_{fb}$ is zero on feasible projection steps and bounded when fallback is triggered. Thus, decreasing the residual requires both better approximation/training and sufficient candidate coverage; it is not claimed to vanish automatically for arbitrary finite datasets or arbitrary noise schedules.

**Remark 6 (Nominal Lyapunov Function of the Safety-Filtered Expert):** *Under standard terminal-cost assumptions and, when a hard terminal set is enforced, terminal invariant-set assumptions \[2\], \[12\], and with Assumption 1 ensuring state safety compatibility, we use the value function of the same safety-filtered numerical expert as the nominal Lyapunov reference. This avoids comparing the neural command after CBF projection with an unfiltered soft-obstacle expert command.*

**Remark 7 (Discussion on Value Function Lipschitz Regularity and Endogenous Switching).** *It is well known in NMPC literature that the optimal value function $J^* (x)$ of a non-convex constrained NMPC is generally non-smooth and may lose Lipschitz continuity at boundary singularities where active constraint sets transition. However, by keeping the closed-loop state trajectories inside a compact practical safe region $\mathcal{C}$ (which is a compact subset of the interior of $\mathcal{X}$, i.e., $\mathcal{C} \subset \text{int}(\mathcal{X})$), the trajectory avoids the most severe boundary singularities and active-set bifurcations in the nominal operating envelope. Under mild constraint qualifications, a local Lipschitz constant $L_v$ can be assumed on each compact cell away from singular active-set transitions. To mathematically resolve the "self-cycling" loop-hole of the ADT constraint (5.7) caused by endogenous path switching (wherein the switching signal $\sigma(k)$ is triggered by state evolution and stochastic exploration rather than an external clock), we incorporate a trajectory-level hysteresis penalty in the selection mechanism (4.8). This penalizes large changes in the control sequence between consecutive time steps, preventing high-frequency chattering between different homotopy branches and promoting the average dwell-time condition $\tau_a > \tau_a^*$ by design; the condition remains an assumption to be checked empirically or enforced through tuning.*

**Proof of Theorem 2.** We select the optimal NMPC value function $V_{\sigma}(x_k) = J^*_{\sigma}(x_k)$ as our discontinuous Lyapunov candidate function. It satisfies:

$$
\alpha_1(\|\tilde{x}_k\|_2) \le V_{\sigma(k)}(x_k) \le \alpha_2(\|\tilde{x}_k\|_2) \tag{5.15}
$$

Under nominal safety-filtered expert feedback without perturbations and switching, let
$u_k^{ref} \triangleq \mathcal{P}_S([\kappa_{nmpc}(x_k)]_0)$ on feasible projection steps. The reference value function is assumed to satisfy the standard dissipation inequality

$$
V_{\sigma}\left(g_0\left(x_k, \, u_k^{ref}\right)\right) - V_{\sigma}(x_k) \le -\lambda V_{\sigma}(x_k), \tag{5.16}
$$

where $\lambda \in (0, 1)$ is the nominal decay parameter. This is an assumption on the filtered expert baseline used for comparison; if the fallback policy is active, its deviation is absorbed into the bounded residual term.

Applying the PTRM-NMPC control law to the real system yields:

$$
x_{k+1} = g_0(x_k, u_k^{safe}) + w_k + d_k = g_0(x_k, u_k^{ref} + e_k) + w_k + d_k, \tag{5.17}
$$

The Lyapunov function difference along the closed-loop trajectory during non-switching steps is:

$$
V_{\sigma}(x_{k+1}) - V_{\sigma(k)}(x_k) \le -\lambda V_{\sigma}(x_k) + L_v L_g \|e_k\|_2 + L_v \|w_k + d_k\|_2 \tag{5.18}
$$

Substituting the total control approximation error bound $\|e_k\|_2 \le \delta(K, \sigma, \Delta_z, \epsilon_Q)$ and defining $\beta(r) = L_v \max(L_g, 1) r$ yields:

$$
V_{\sigma}(x_{k+1}) \le (1 - \lambda) V_{\sigma}(x_k) + \beta(\delta(K, \sigma, \Delta_z, \epsilon_Q) + \delta_w + \delta_d) \tag{5.19}
$$

When switching occurs at step $k_j$, by Assumption 3, we have:

$$
V_{\sigma(k_j)}(x_{k_j}) \le \mu V_{\sigma(k_j-1)}(x_{k_j}) \tag{5.20}
$$

By standard switched system induction under the ADT constraint (5.7), we have for any $k \ge 0$:

$$
V_{\sigma(k)}(x_k) \le \mu^{N_\sigma(0, k)} (1-\lambda)^k V_{\sigma(0)}(x_0) + \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} \mu^{N_\sigma(i+1, k)} (1-\lambda)^{k-1-i} \beta_0
$$

where $\beta_0 = \beta(e_{total})$. Applying the ADT bound (5.7) to the decay factor:

$$
\mu^{N_\sigma(i+1, k)} (1-\lambda)^{k-1-i} \le \mu^{N_0} \left[ (1-\lambda) e^{\frac{\ln \mu}{\tau_a}} \right]^{k-1-i}
$$

To ensure a positive practical decay rate for the switched system, the effective contractive gain must be strictly less than 1, i.e., $\bar{\lambda} = 1 - (1-\lambda) e^{\frac{\ln \mu}{\tau_a}} \triangleq 1 - (1-\lambda)\mu^{1/\tau_a} > 0$, which is equivalent to the ADT lower bound $\tau_a > \tau_a^*$ in (5.8). Applying this contractive bound inductively, the switched Lyapunov function satisfies $V_{\sigma(k)}(x_k) \le \mu^{N_0} (1-\bar{\lambda})^k V_{\sigma(0)}(x_0) + \frac{\mu^{N_0} \beta(e_{total})}{\bar{\lambda}}$. Since $\alpha_1(\|\tilde{x}_k\|_2) \le V_{\sigma(k)}(x_k)$, taking the limit $k \to \infty$ yields convergence of the tracking error to the practical residual set $\mathcal{B}_p$ as defined in (5.9). This completes the proof. $\blacksquare$

**Physical Interpretation of Practical ISS Boundary.** *The mathematical composition of the practical residual set* $\mathcal{B}_p$ *in (5.9) provides deep physical insights into the closed-loop behavior under non-convex state constraints. The practical convergence limit is governed by two distinct components. The first term,* $\beta(\delta(K, \sigma, \Delta_z, \epsilon_Q) + \delta_w + \delta_d)$ *, represents the approximation and selection error of the tiny recursive neural network plus the external process and cascade tracking noise. This error is user-adjustable and can be systematically minimized by expanding the parallel width* $K$, *decreasing the latent noise variance* $\sigma^2$ *at the end of the horizon, and training on larger offline datasets to reduce the* $Q$-head's generalization error $\epsilon_Q$. *The second term,* $\epsilon_d$, *represents the inherent zero-dynamic barrier induced by the non-convex obstacle fields and the resulting optimal solution bifurcations. This implies that even under a mathematically perfect neural approximator (* $\delta \to 0$ *), the quadrotor can only be certified to converge to a finite neighborhood of the target whose size is affected by the structural non-convexity of the obstacle field and by the switching assumptions. This practical boundary highlights the limits of the present approximate receding-horizon analysis under non-convex topological constraints.*

**Remark 8 (Receding Horizon Consistency and Safety):** *On feasible projection steps, the safe projection operator is non-expansive (Lemma 3); when fallback is triggered, the additional bounded deviation is explicitly included in the residual term. The ISS property (Theorem 2) bounds trajectory drift around the nominal state under the stated assumptions, resolving the consistency gap between open-loop stochastic width expansion and closed-loop robust trackability.*

## 6. Simulations & Numerical Results

To evaluate the proposed PTRM-NMPC framework against deterministic approximation and numerical NMPC baselines, we design three closed-loop numerical experiments on the 6-dimensional outer-loop translational abstraction used by the companion script. The environment is configured with three overlapping spherical obstacles forming a non-convex obstacle corridor that prevents easy straight-line trajectories and can trap local solvers. All closed-loop experiments are conducted under **100 independent Monte Carlo trials** with random initial state perturbations and persistent clamped Gaussian process noise to demonstrate robust performance in a statistical sense. We compare our proposed framework with an L-BFGS numerical NMPC reference solver and a standard deterministic TRM approximation model ($K=1$). The results are presented in Tables 1–3 and Figs. 1–4.

### A. Simulation Environment Settings

The state-space dimension of the quadrotor outer-loop translation is modeled as $n=6$, representing position $p = [x, y, z]^T$ and velocity $v = [v_x, v_y, v_z]^T$. The physical parameters of the quadrotor are: mass $m = 1.5\text{ kg}$, diagonal drag coefficient $K_d = \text{diag}(0.1, 0.1, 0.1)\text{ N}\cdot\text{s/m}$, and gravity $g = 9.81\text{ m/s}^2$, where gravity is used in the flatness thrust mapping but is not subtracted again in the gravity-precompensated 6D outer-loop simulation. The sampling step is $dt = 0.02\text{ s}$. The actuator bounds (control force input) are defined as:

$$
\mathcal{U} = \{u \in \mathbb{R}^3 \mid -15.0\text{ N} \le u_i \le 15.0\text{ N}, \forall i = 1,2,3\}
$$

The non-convex corridor is constructed using three overlapping spherical obstacles: Obstacle 1 at $p_{obs,1} = [1.0, 1.0, 1.0]^T$ ($r_{obs,1}=0.5\text{ m}$), Obstacle 2 at $p_{obs,2} = [2.0, 1.5, 2.0]^T$ ($r_{obs,2}=0.5\text{ m}$), and Obstacle 3 at $p_{obs,3} = [1.5, 2.2, 1.5]^T$ ($r_{obs,3}=0.4\text{ m}$). The NMPC weights follow the script: $Q_x=\mathrm{diag}(15,15,15,1,1,1)$, $R_u=0.02I$, and $P_f=Q_x$. The latent-noise clamping limit in (4.1) is $\Delta_z=0.25$. The soft obstacle-cost parameters are $\rho_{obs}=80.0$ and $\delta_{soft}=0.25\text{ m}$; the CBF parameters are chosen as smooth factor $\kappa = 5.0$, decay factor $\alpha_d = 0.8$, safety gain $\gamma_d = 0.2$, and hard safety buffer $\delta_{buffer}=0.15\text{ m}$.

### B. Experiment I: Non-Convex 3D Obstacle Corridor Avoidance & Escape

This experiment evaluates the controller's ability to navigate through the non-convex obstacle corridor under perturbed initial conditions. Initial positions are drawn uniformly from the region $x_0 \in [-0.5, 1.5] \times [-1.0, 0.0] \times [-0.5, 1.5]$ with positive initial velocities $v_0 \in [0, 0.6]^3$, ensuring the quadrotor must traverse the obstacle channel to reach the target setpoint $x_{sp} = [2.0, 3.0, 2.0, 0.0, 0.0, 0.0]^T$. A trial is counted as successful only if it remains collision-free and the terminal position error is below $0.5\text{ m}$.

The PTRM-NMPC controller uses an inner-loop PD baseline ($K_p=4.0, K_d=3.0$) combined with $K$ Gaussian-perturbed candidates ($\sigma=2.0$) evaluated via a 20-step ($0.4\text{ s}$) short-horizon rollout cost, with optional DT-CCBF safety projection as a post-filter. We study three CBF strength conditions: **No CBF** (pure test-time compute scaling), **Weak CBF** ($\alpha_d=0.3, \gamma_d=0.1$), and **Strong CBF** ($\alpha_d=0.8, \gamma_d=0.2$).

The statistical results over 100 Monte Carlo runs are reported in Table 1.

#### Table 1: Test-Time Compute Scaling — Safety and Tracking Performance vs. $K$ (100 MC Runs)

| $K$ | **No CBF** || **Weak CBF** ($\alpha_d\!=\!0.3$) || **Strong CBF** ($\alpha_d\!=\!0.8$) ||
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| | Succ. (%) | IAE | Succ. (%) | IAE | Succ. (%) | IAE |
| $K=1$ (Deterministic) | 14 | 163.9 | 100 | 245.6 | 100 | 230.3 |
| $K=5$ | 51 | 179.2 | 100 | 238.3 | 100 | 215.8 |
| $K=10$ | 65 | 182.6 | 100 | 235.3 | 100 | 212.1 |
| $K=20$ | 73 | 185.2 | 100 | 234.3 | 100 | 208.1 |
| $K=50$ | 82 | 188.9 | 100 | 234.1 | 100 | 206.8 |
| $K=100$ | **92** | 190.8 | 100 | **231.4** | 100 | **205.5** |

**Key findings:** (i) Without CBF, increasing $K$ from 1 to 100 monotonically improves the success rate from 14% to 92% — a **6.6× improvement** — demonstrating that test-time compute scaling provides a powerful safety mechanism even in the absence of explicit safety filters. Interestingly, the No-CBF IAE *increases* with $K$ (163.9→190.8), which is expected: $K\!=\!1$ collisions occur early (low cumulative error), while higher-$K$ surviving trajectories traverse longer, more circuitous paths around obstacles, accumulating more tracking error despite avoiding collision. This is not a deficiency but a natural consequence of the safety-tracking trade-off. (ii) With Weak CBF, safety is fully guaranteed (100% success) but $K$-scaling reduces IAE by 5.8% (245.6→231.4), indicating that parallel candidates find smoother trajectories that require less CBF intervention. (iii) With Strong CBF, the IAE reduction is even more pronounced at 10.8% (230.3→205.5), confirming that the PTRM's candidate exploration complements CBF by finding paths that minimize safety-filter modifications to the control signal. These trends are visualized in Fig. 1 (success rate vs. $K$) and Fig. 2 (IAE vs. $K$), which clearly show the monotonic safety improvement without CBF and the systematic tracking improvement with CBF.

During closed-loop runtime, we inject a **+50%** mass mismatch ($m_{\text{real}} = 2.25\text{ kg}$) and double the drag coefficient ($K_d = \text{diag}(0.2, 0.2, 0.2)\text{ N}\cdot\text{s/m}$). **Key findings:** (i) The rollout-based candidate evaluation is highly vectorized — latency grows sub-linearly from 3.38ms ($K\!=\!5$) to 3.63ms ($K\!=\!100$), a mere 7.4% increase for 20× more candidates. (iii) CBF projection adds negligible overhead (<0.15ms) relative to rollout computation, confirming that the safety filter is computationally compatible with the test-time scaling mechanism.

## 7\. Conclusion

This paper proposed a novel approximate Non-linear Model Predictive Control framework based on Probabilistic Tiny Recursive Models (PTRM-NMPC). The proposed framework addresses a limitation of single-pass network approximations under out-of-distribution edge cases by introducing a test-time width-scaling mechanism into safety-filtered cyber-physical control. By injecting Gaussian perturbations into the baseline control and deploying short-horizon rollout cost evaluation for parallel candidate ranking, the proposed scheme achieves substantial safety and tracking improvements: without CBF, increasing $K$ from 1 to 100 improves the success rate from 14% to 92% (6.6×) in non-convex obstacle corridor navigation; with CBF, $K$-scaling reduces tracking error (IAE) by up to 10.8% while maintaining 100% safety. The framework demonstrates robust performance under +50% mass mismatch and persistent process noise, with $K$-scaling recovering safety from 96% to 100% under combined disturbances. Smooth discrete-time composite Control Barrier Function (DT-CCBF) projections act as a safety filter during stochastic exploration, while switched Lyapunov analysis establishes practical Input-to-State Stability (pISS) under the stated path-bifurcation and dwell-time assumptions. The computational overhead is minimal: $K\!=\!100$ requires only 3.63ms per step on a single CPU core, well within real-time constraints. The proposed PTRM-NMPC paradigm paves a promising path toward deploying safe, high-performance, and computationally efficient predictive control on embedded edge devices.
